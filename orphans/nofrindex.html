Forty-eight of the best minds from the United States and abroad have been convened by University of Pennsylvania President Judith Rodin to take a careful look at the contemporary explosion of intolerance, ideological extremism, and incivility. Leading academics, writers, legal experts, social scientists, politicians, and journalists, including several Penn faculty, are working together over three years to understand the origins of these phenomena and develop new ideas, approaches and "ways of thinking differently" about contemporary issues that can reduce the tendency to polarize every issue and demonize every opponent.

The Commission's general goal is to promote a robust and diverse public culture in which reasoned and reasonable discourse can flourish.

The Commission arose out of a desire to demonstrate that the knowledge and experience of our professional and scholarly communities, both in and outside the academy, can be brought productively to bear on major social and cultural problems as we begin to confront the complex challenges of the next century. Prominent among these problems is the need, both in the U.S. and abroad, to create a more genuinely engaged and productive public culture in which a reasoned and reasonable discourse (though not necessarily a more "civil" one) is possible, despite disruptive, market-driven, behavioral patterns. In such a culture, leadership and expertise would play a useful and appropriate role within the context of democratic values, and a sense of shared community would frame and facilitate energetic engagement about our real and valuable disagreements over policies, politics, values, and other complex issues. Rather than quashing debate or discord, the need is to foster a more enthusiastic and substantive engagement across all kinds of differences.

With this general framework in mind, the Commission is exploring and addressing three major problem areas:

  • The Culture of Intolerance

  • The Failure of Leadership

  • The Fragmentation of Communities.

Each problem area is the focus of a working group of Commission members as well as a future plenary meeting of the Commission. From these activities a variety of publications, research findings, initiatives, and other materials will emerge. Rather than issue a massive report and make recommendations for others to act upon, the Commission hopes in this way to generate and share broadly a body of ideas that will help to foster a more reasoned and reasonable public discourse within a more robust and diverse public culture.

Members of the Commission represent many different disciplines, professions, political perspectives, and opinions. The Commission is chaired by Dr. Judith Rodin, President of the University of Pennsylvania, and professor of psychology, medicine, and psychiatry.

    The University of Pennsylvania is one of the world's great international research universities, with special strengths in the behavioral sciences, American history and cultural studies, communications, law, business, community service, and social policy. Penn's historic and identifying institutional characteristic has been its commitment to unifying theory and practice, which dates back to the educational philosophy of its founder, Benjamin Franklin, and led to many of the University's historic "firsts" and its current strengths in professional education in medicine, business, law, etc., as well as the arts and sciences. These resources make Penn a natural home for such a unique undertaking as the Penn National Commission on Society, Culture and Community. The Commission's formation, under the leadership of Penn President Judith Rodin, expresses the University's fundamental commitments to academic excellence, to the betterment of our society, to technological innovation in research and scholarly/professional communication, and to bringing the resources of our international academic and professional communities to bear on urgent social problems.
