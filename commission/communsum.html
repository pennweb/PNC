SUMMARY OF FIRST MEETING (6/12/97)
 by William D. Boltz

The Working Group on 21st Century Community of the Penn National Commission on Society, Culture and Community began its work by conducting a wide ranging discussion on the nature and meaning of community. Issues of popular culture, values, civic spaces, and the arts, and their relationships to concepts of community were all placed on the table. The group concluded by outlining a series of questions to be investigated and tasks to be performed. These questions and tasks will be undertaken in a three step process of exploration, definition and recommendation.

Exploration

The first task for the group will be to investigate the types of communities which exist or are emerging today. This will be done through approaches to states' humanities councils, surveys of the internet, and communication with other groups working in this area.

The group will look at the goals communities pursue and the tasks they set for themselves. Members expressed interest in exploring communities of purpose and affinity, as well as distinguishing those seeking positive goals from those which exist for negative purposes (e.g., lynch mobs.) Examples of successful and unsuccessful communities will be sought; points of commonality and difference will be determined. This will allow the group to develop models of community which will be the basis for much of its later work.

During this phase, the working group will also seek to enlarge its membership by going outside the confines of the PNC. Expanding the diversity of the group was seen as essential for the success of the group's efforts.

Definition

 Having acquired data and developed models, the group will seek definitions for several key concepts and answers to major questions. For example, what are the preconditions for community? Among possible answers offered at the meeting were affection and common purpose or common values, myths and norms.

A quality of openness to other groups was recognized as a positive feature for subcommunities and communities operating in a democratic society. However, others noted that for some groups a degree of exclusivity is central to the achievement of their goals. What is the proper relationship between the need for communal exclusivity and openness to others?

What can be learned from racially or ethnically based communities? Can successes found in these groups be used by others trying to forge creative linkages across such divides?

Communities that play a public role, either deliberative or active, will be especially important to the group as well as determining how more private communities affect the larger civic community.

Recommendations

The working group will initiate approaches in different areas. Four specific areas emerged from the discussions:

  1. Internet

    The internet is forcing us all to rethink many of the central notions of community such as geographical proximity and temporal commitment. It is acting as a wellspring for new and often spontaneous communities. At the same time, it is feeding a culture of misinformation, negativity, and incivility. The working group will seek concrete ways to make the internet a creative force for building and sustaining community.

  2. "New Communities"

    In addition to the internet, new forms of community are emerging all around us. The group will actively seek out groups, both in the US and abroad, which are seeking to form new communities through unconventional techniques. The role the arts can play in bringing people from diverse backgrounds together will be emphasized.

  3. Universities

    University communities were held up as exemplars of the challenges to and opportunities for contemporary community. The significant issues facing institutions of higher education (e.g., the canon, affirmative action, fragmentation of the student body, housing) are all at base questions of community.

  4. "Community of Citizens"

    In this phase, the group will also address the relationship between communities and the larger "community of citizenship." Ways in which communities might act to create civic spaces for the discussion of public issues and the re-engagement of citizens in political processes will be explored, developed, and promoted.
Questions for Discussion
  1. Are there any corrections, additions, or deletions you would make to this summary?

  2. What would be the best way for the group to increase its diversity ?

  3. Would it be useful, at this stage, for the group to commission research? If so, what should be the focus of this initial research?

  4. The group is scheduled to present its findings at the plenary meeting to be held in Los Angeles in December 1998. What sort of timetable should the group set up for its work?

  5. Creating new communities through electronic communication will be one of the focuses of this group's investigations. As we work together to form our own community, largely via the listserv, are there ways you think would be useful in making this a more meaningful and successful experience?
