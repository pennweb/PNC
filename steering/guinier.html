December 9, 1996
Penn National Commission

Thinning of Social Discourse / Silence on Race

Presentation by Professor Lani Guinier

LANI GUINIER: I'm delighted, actually, to follow the historical narrative with a more particular narrative, and I'm delighted to follow the historical narrative with a more particular narrative that takes place in this law school, because part of what I would like to add to what we've already heard is the role of lawyers and legal culture in framing public discourse.

And I'd like to start, actually, from a vision. That is, I'd like to start from a class that takes place in this law school that is co-taught by Professor Susan Sterm and myself. It's a class on race and gender. It's a class of 20 students who must apply to be admitted. It is an extraordinarily diverse class. We just finished teaching this class last Wednesday and the last class, as is our tradition, takes place at my house. And although the class is supposed to take two hours, they didn't leave my house--they came at 5 and they didn't leave my house until almost 11, which is typical of this class. And I think in part the fact that the students don't want to leave the class is one of the most important virtues and one of the most important markers of the success of this class. And I think if we're going to start from a positive vision, that is: What would public discourse--what would a group of people committed to working together and trying to solve public problems look like and how would we know it when we saw it?

One of the first things I think we would see is sustained communication and a desire to continue the conversation so that the mark of a successful conversation, the mark of successful public discourse, is a commitment to come back. It is not something that you can measure simply by an outcome, it is really a commitment to a process. And the fact that these students didn't want to leave my house, even though one of them had an exam the very next day, which probably doesn't speak too well of my priorities or hers, but the fact that they didn't want to leave my house, and the fact that as we went around the room and asked everyone to debrief--and this, again, was another innovation. Most of the time in law school classes when people do debriefing and give faculty an evaluation, they do it anonymously. They do it on a piece of paper and they don't give the feedback face-to-face. This was a public debriefing in that everyone went around the room and described what it is that they most valued about the class.

And in that debriefing, several of the students said, `This is a class that reminded me why I went to law school. This is a class that restored my sense of self. This is a class that gave me back the private space to be who I am.' And, `This is a class which I hope I will be able to continue having contact with even after today is over.' That is, `I know that 20 years from now when I'm having a problem in my law practice, I will be able to call one of the members of this class up and we will be able to brainstorm together.'

Now what is most amazing about these sentiments is that this is a class, as I said, that is enormously diverse. There were four black students in the class, including one who described herself as a black nationalist. There were four Asian students. There were two East Indian students. There were three Latinos. There were seven whites, including three Jewish students, one of whom is Orthodox. So this is a very diverse group of people, and they said this now was their friends. These were people that they had learned to respect as a result of coming together.

Now how did we make this diverse group of students feel like friends? And Susan Sterm and I are lawyers. We're law professors. We are not historians, we are not philosophers, we are not sociologists. But one of the things that I think we did that is most important, not just in terms of having the class and providing space for this class, but we turned over responsibility for conducting the class to the students. And I think this is a very important aspect of positive or affirmative vision, going back to the critique of expertise, the critique of elitism and the notion that part of the problem with our present public discourse is a sense that there are people in charge who are those with the knowledge and the information who are going to fix things and then the rest of us sit around and observe passively. [T]he rest of us are the spectators and the professors, the governors, the people in charge are the sage on the stage.

Well, what we did in this class is we reversed that and gave most of the power and responsibility to the students who each had to facilitate two of the classes. And in their facilitation, not only did they have the responsibility for leading the class discussion, but they also had the opportunity--and this comes back to one of the last points that Tom Bender made--they had the opportunity to work very closely with each other, because no one facilitated the class alone. They were at least two and generally three students who were facilitating. And in that project of having a class that they were responsible for facilitating, they learned how to work together.

They also engaged in multiple forms of participation. This was not just students standing up and replacing the professor and talking at another group of students. They were engaged in role playing. They had to write reflection pieces. This was an opportunity for people to participate in many different forms and I think that's another aspect of this, quote, "affirmative vision." Because some people don't do very well if the only way that they can speak is through a performance, or where they have to speak in a very loud voice.

The other thing that we emphasized in this class was the value of difference. This class was on race and gender, but this was not about transcending difference. This was about respecting and working with difference. And as we went around the room and the students debriefed, each of them thanked all of the other students in the class for being so different. That is, for teaching them not only about the other students but about themselves, for reminding them that their own perspectives were partial and while deeply held, did not explain the universe for everyone else in the class.

The final thing that we did that I would like to mark or signal as an affirmative or a positive vision is that we constructed the class as a problem-solving class. We did not see this class simply as an opportunity to talk. This was not a class in which people simply talked about their personal experiences.

This was not a class in which we talked only abstractly about the law. This was a class in which we continuously moved from the personal to the abstract and then back again, all the while trying very hard to work together through various problems that had been put on the table as a result of the facilitation.

Now what I'd like to do, if this class represents a positive vision, it represents an intermediate space where people from very different backgrounds could come together and constructively and happily work together, how can we strategize backwards? How can we take that class and use it as a critique of what most of us do in public life?

I think if we look at that class as--or at least, for me-- a positive vision and then move backwards to see what is it that we're doing in our public life that makes it impossible for us to engage in problem solving in a way that that class did, I think we see that it's not an accident that we're meeting here in the law school. It's not an accident that we're meeting here in the law school, because in many ways, what legal culture has done is to construct a very adversarial view of public conversation. And what legal culture has done is to provide the metaphor for much of public conversation. And the metaphor is that you have two opposing parties who fight with each other but don't really interact with each other because they are in a courtroom in which they are fighting against each other to impress a third party--the judge or the referee--who then scores them; who then determines who has won.

And if you think about this model of truth detection as it is derived from the courtroom, you see a model in which people are not talking to each other, in which people are not trying to solve problems together, but, in fact, they are encouraged to perform. This is what my colleague calls the gladiator phenomena.

And when we think about public conversation as a performance, in that gladiator model, then each single event in the conversation assumes enormous importance. Because each of us is being judged by our performance. And as a result, many people fear speaking aloud, or when they do speak aloud, they're not speaking to anyone but the group or the side that they are already working with or associated with. When they speak aloud, they are speaking not to resolve a problem but to win because, again, this is all about gamesmanship. And the focus is not on the process. The focus is not, was this a successful conversation? Do we want to continue this conversation? The focus is exclusively on the outcome. Who won this conversation and who lost? And in that process, we discourage people from wanting to come back.

And so, again, looking at the class as the model of a successful conversation--meaning a conversation that we want to continue--most public conversation is a disaster, because we do not want it to continue. Those of us who have been exposed to public conversation are happiest when we are far away from it.

Now I don't mean to say that the adversarial model is the exclusive culprit here, and I think that the media reinforces much of what is wrong with the adversarial truth-detection model. As Cornell West and Michael Lerner described the role of the media in an article they wrote, ÒJews and Blacks and Dialogue,Ó they say, `The media consistently attempts,' and I quote, "to fit our public dialogue into its own master discourse that highlights the tensions and underplays the solidarity." And again, this is an ironic confluence where the media, the journalists and the lawyers are coming together to create a dramatic moment and they are coming together but the rest of us are coming apart, because they are each focusing on identifying the opposing sides to a conflict and they are both arriving at the same destination, which essentially is a ritualized contest between dichotomized and individualized positions.

Now there are other ways in which adversarial or legal models of problem solving have infiltrated our public culture to our detriment. Tom Bender talked about the role of experts. I think lawyers reinforce that notion that there's a hierarchy and that the lawyers are the only ones who are allowed to speak and everyone else is supposed to listen or watch. Lawyers and legal culture tends to emphasize that there is a universal, a single standard, the law, by which everyone must be judged and as a result it de-emphasizes or minimizes the role of context, which is often very important in terms of trying to resolve various problems. And in this case, race is a very good example of the problem of using a universal or single standard to try and resolve problems, because we tend to think of race in public as a binary category. You're either black or you're white; you're either a racist or you're not a racist.

Race is a much more fluid and much more complicated and much more nuanced issue. And let me just give you an example of this, again, coming from the class. There was a young woman in the class who was objecting to the conversation about race because she is a Jewish student and she felt that when we were talking about race we should also be talking about Jews, because for her, being Jewish was a race. And so this created a very interesting conversation among many of the Jewish students in the class, whether they agreed with her that being Jewish was a race. And then one of the students said, `Well, when you're talking about being a race, do you mean that you agree with some of the callers who call up the NAACP Legal Defense Fund in New York and when they're asked to report on a particular discrimination problem, they say, "Was your employer black or white." And some of them say, "No, neither. They were Jewish." Is that what you mean by saying, "Being Jewish is a race?"' And the woman said, `Oh, absolutely not.' That's not what she meant.

What she was trying to do was to introduce a nuance into the conversation without being trapped into a binary category. And part of the problem with our public conversation about race is that we don't allow people to do that. If she is going to say being Jewish is a race, then it must be a race for all times and in all cases. And so part of the challenge is to figure out ways in which we can talk about something as complicated and as contextualized as race without being trapped into a universal or one-size-fits-all standard.

Now the other problem with talking or using the adversarial model in public conversation is that, again, one size fits all and a universal standard suggests that fault or blame are the only ways in which we can assess public conversation about race. They are the currency of moral authority: Fault or blame. Again, that's the imposition of this adversarial metaphor.

And so an individual who is talking about race is either a good guy or a bad guy. They're either responsible for all of the problems of the world or they're innocent of all of the problems of the world. And we don't see issues of race as having a class dimension, as having a gender dimension, as having a systemic dimension. It's all individualized and it's all as if this were a criminal case. And when someone speaks and they make a mistake or they speak out of turn, then we have to put them on trial. We indict them and we have to prosecute them. We bring in character witnesses and we have to determine whether in fact they can be absolved of their racial fault. And the naming and then blaming framework simply increases the risk of speaking out publicly. And as a result, people are afraid to talk out loud about issues such as race.

Well, let me just conclude by coming back to the class and suggesting a way in which this class is able to solve a problem using a very different, less-polarized, less-adversarial model and not simply talking about race in a crisis situation and not simply talking about race through a one-size-fits-all lens. And the contrast is the case of Lowell High School in California. And the contrast is that when I was on a book tour on talk radio, I got a lot of calls--and I was in San Francisco--from people who said, `Well, what do you think of Lowell High School?' And I had to have an answer--Right?--because this is talk radio, this is the name and blame game. `Is Lowell High School right, is it wrong? Do you side with the Chinese-American students or with the NAACP?'

The facts of Lowell High School are that like all San Francisco public high schools, Lowell High School was under a consent decree to ensure that no one ethnic or racial group was more than 40 percent of the population at any San Francisco high school. And as a result of that consent decree to desegregate the San Francisco schools, the school, which is a magnet public school -- it boasts a number of distinguished alums including Stephen J. Breyer -- the school had a test for admission--69 was a perfect score--and they were using a sliding scale to determine who could get in in order to maintain diversity at the school. And if you were Chinese-American, initially you had to score 65 out of 69 in order to get in. If you were Asian-American, but not Chinese-American or you were white, you had to get 62 out of 69 in order to get in. If you were African-American or Mexican-American, you had to get a 59 out of 69 in order to get in.

So a group of Chinese-American parents intervened to try to reopen this consent decree, saying that the sliding scale discriminated against their children. And a group of African-American parents and the NAACP said, `No, if we don't have this sliding scale, we will not have diversity at Lowell High School.' And so there was a conflict between the NAACP and the Chinese-American parents. And that's the conflict into which I walked when I was on talk radio in San Francisco.

Well, my solution to that problem was to say, `I'll get back to you.' And I took that problem to the class on race and gender at the University of Pennsylvania Law School. And I said, `OK. Here's the problem. I don't want an answer that tells me who's right and who's wrong. I don't want a vote in which we decide that the Chinese-Americans get this many votes and the NAACP gets that many votes. I want us to talk about what are the underlying assumptions behind each side's claim and how could we, if we were running Lowell High School, change the admissions procedure to accommodate the legitimate concerns of everybody on both sides of the debate, or at least more people on both sides of the debate.' And we came up with some very interesting approaches.

One of the students said, `Well, they should have a lottery. If 59 is the lowest score by which they admit in anybody, then take everyone with a 59 on this test and put their names in a hat and just pull the names out of the hat using random selection.' And another student said, `Well, but the school may have certain things that it values, not just how well you do on this test.' So they have a conversation and suggested if the school, in conjunction with the community and the alums and the present students, determines that there are other things that they value in addition to how you do on this test and those things may be as far-reaching as whether you play trombone and they need trombone players or whether they're looking for a very diverse group of people in terms of ZIP codes or in terms of what your major may be, then if, as a community, they determine that they are committed to these other features and there are students who have those other characteristics, play the trombone, who are likely, because of past success, show promise of being a Supreme Court justice or of winning some science finals or science fellowship, put their names in twice or put their names in three times to enhance the chances that they will be selected.

Some students said, `Well, why are we focused--why are we allowing Lowell High School to use this test? What do we know about this test? Does this test predict any success or is it just an arbitrary way of screening people? Maybe they should not allow anybody to go to Lowell High School for four years and instead they should have people come through the school for a year. If it's such a valuable resource, why should they limit it only to those students who score well on a particular test?'

They came up with many, many different solutions. The point of this exercise is not that they came up with the right answer. The point of this exercise is that they came up with a really helpful, creative process. A group of very diverse people with very different, deeply held beliefs were able to work together and to work through a problem in ways that each of them could respect the views and the approaches of people on very different sides of the podium.

I gave a similar talk to the United Methodist Church and a middle-aged man raised his hand after I was finished and he said, `I know exactly what you are talking about. It's what my wife has been telling me for 30 years of marriage.' I said, `Well, that's great. What is it?' And he said, `My wife has been telling me that when we fight, I fight to win and she fights to resolve.' And I think if there's anything about this positive vision from this class, it is fighting--it is disagreeing to resolve, not to win.

Thank you very much.
